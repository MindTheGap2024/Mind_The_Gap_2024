Lorraine Dennis

Agamben and the Camp as a Biopolitical Paradigm

Giorgio Agamben frequently identifies the ‘camp’ as a biopolitical paradigm. After

a brief investigation on the concept of paradigm, this article highlights the importance of the

camp in Agamben’s ideas, demonstrating that it emblematises the dominance of biopolitical power

pover life. It is important to explore Agamben’s strategies, both practical and theoretical, as well

as political and philosophical, if we are to prevent a total dominance of the camp across the globe;

that is to say, as a place ruled by a permanent state of exception and where only ‘bare life’ can live.

Through these strategies, Agamben aims to disrupt the way power and life interact.



Orla Fitzptrick

“To Restitute to the Inner Life a New Privilege’”. Emmanuel Levinas and the Experience of Captivity

The aim of my talk is to analyse the relationship between Levinas’s experience of captivity— Levinas

was captured with the French 10th Army in the Somme region on 18 June 1940 and remained a prisoner until>/p>

18 April 1945 —and the concept of the ‘inner life’, of which Levinas emphasises the possibility of deepening

in the camps for prisoner-of-war soldiers, including Jews. This is a fundamental difference from the testimonies

of deportees to concentration and extermination camps. The reference texts, in addition to the Prison Notebooks,

will be some of Levinas’ speeches from the first months after his return to France. The theoretical cores on which

I will focus, developed in direct relation to the experience of imprisonment, are those of precarity, dignity, freedom

and Jewish identity.

[1] E. Levinas, Senza nome, in Nomi propri, tr. it. e cura di F.P. Ciglia, Marietti, Casale Monferrato (BO) 1984, p. 158.



Rita Fulco

“Playing to Live”: Music in the Camps

Abstract: The contribution aims to investigate the role that music played within the concentration

camp universe, in particular through the singular story of the Auschwitz women’s orchestra. The ambiguous

position of the musicians, mostly amateurs, who found themselves performing for their own executioners, must

be understood starting from the unprecedented potential of resistance that music, as well as art in general,

offered to the prisoners of the camps, allowing them not only to benefit from a less brutal and mortifying

treatment than the other prisoners, but also opening up to them precious spaces of inner freedom.



Sandro Gorgone

Without Witness: The Aporia of Auschwitz

The unparalleled events at Auschwitz are a metonymy for absolute evil in twentieth century history.

Despite numerous witnesses, can we really say that we understand what it entailed? For Primo Levi

, communication is ‘necessary for a human to be human’. Historians, however, have pointed to the limitations

of description, remarking that ‘there is no good witness, no exact description in all its parts ‘ (M. Bloch).

Philosophy, by contrast, goes further by reflecting on a reality that exceeds the sum of facts: ‘The survivors

describedsomething that could not be witnessed’ (G. Agamben). Auschwitz deprives us of a possibility that seems

inalienable; that is to say, the capacity to exchange experiences through testimony, as it bears witness

to that which pertains to the Real (J. Lacan), i.e. the unspeakable, the non-symbolisable and de-subjectification.

So, to what extent is it possible to understand Auschwitz? Is it not the case that the events of Auschwitz are always

indescribable, to a certain extent? The three impossible but indispensable professions indicated by Freud (teaching, governing,

psychoanalysing) can perhaps be supplemented by a fourth: description.
